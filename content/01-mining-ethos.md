# Mining Ethos

Mining ORE is not a slot machine. It is a patience game wrapped in a mining game.

The strongest voices in the channel converge on the same idea: if your goal is to build ORE exposure over time, the real product of mining is not the emotional high of one motherlode round. The real product is uORE, yield, conviction, and time.

## Cubs — The refinORE Manifesto

> Mining ORE isn't about winning every round. It's about building a position in the only asset you can't fully buy your way into: uORE. The edge is patience, consistency, and information. That's why I built refinORE the way I did. Not as a "click button, get rich" miner, but as a command center for people who actually want to understand and maintain a mining strategy over time. To me, real mining is not gambling on one lucky tile. It's stacking uORE intelligently, protecting yourself from dumb mistakes, and letting time + yield do the work. ORE rewards patience. refinORE helps you operationalize it.

## The shared ethos

- Mine to accumulate uORE, not to win every board.
- Think in weeks, months, and years — not one round.
- All 25 tiles is mining. Fewer tiles is higher-variance gambling.
- Claiming early has a cost. Patience earns from the impatience of others.
- Production cost matters. If mining cost is far above market price, sometimes buying ORE is cleaner.
- Tiny, consistent deployments beat emotional overdeployment.

## The mantra

> Mine patiently. Claim reluctantly. Let time do the heavy lifting.

## David — The Never-Claim Philosophy

David's ethos is simple and unwavering: mine over time, don't worry about short-term motherlode results or price swings, mine 22-25 squares every round, and never claim uORE. His long-term vision: borrow against uORE someday rather than ever selling.

> The goal isn't to time the market. It's to stack time in the market.

## Fronk — Low and Slow

Fronk embodies the disciplined, conservative miner: tiny miner, 25 blocks every round, always check ore.fyi production cost vs. ORE price before deciding to run. Low-and-slow is the way.

> Mining should be boring. If it's exciting, you're probably doing it wrong.

## Mining vs. Gambling

This is the most important distinction in the ORE mining ethos. 25 tiles/all blocks = mining. Fewer tiles = higher variance/gambling. The community consensus is clear: real miners use all blocks. The math favors consistency over luck.

## The patience equation

ORE makes impatience mathematically expensive:

- Claiming has a 10% fee — every claim costs you a tenth of your uORE
- Patient holders absorb forfeits — when others claim, you benefit
- ~70-75% APR on uORE — time literally pays you to hold
- Compounding is the real strategy — the longer you wait, the more you earn

This isn't just philosophy. It's game theory built into the protocol.

## The mistake this channel is trying to prevent

New miners often arrive asking, "How do I win?"

The better question is: "How do I build a position without getting shaken out by variance?"

That shift — from winning rounds to managing a long-term strategy — is the entire game.

## FiL — Accessibility Over Adrenaline

FiL's story adds an important beginner-friendly frame: ORE works because the core loop is simple enough for regular people to understand. After years of crypto and memecoin whiplash, he sees mining as a calmer discipline where patience matters more than emotional swings.

## Phone-First Access

Suckytrader and Fronk added a simple but important accessibility point: a miner can learn and participate from a phone. Almost everyone has one; not everyone has a desktop or old-school mining hardware.

That does not make mining risk-free, and it does not replace the need for safe settings, production-cost checks, and SOL budgeting. But it does change the story. ORE can be taught as a system ordinary people can touch directly, with the device already in their pocket, before they ever become power users.

> The on-ramp matters. If ORE is understandable and mobile-accessible, it can reach people who were never going to run a GPU rig or chase insider rotations.

## Fronk's mining ethos, preserved as a command

A Carl-bot repost preserved Fronk's cleanest version of the low-and-slow thesis: mining is not supposed to feel immediately profitable every round. The purpose is to collect uORE, the mining-only asset that earns roughly 70-75% APR at current rates, and to do it with enough discipline that variance does not knock the miner out of the game.

Fronk's practical version stays simple: tiny 25-block miners, often around 0.00015 SOL per block, left to run slowly while checking ore.fyi before mining. If production cost is far above the ORE market price, the disciplined move may be to pause and buy instead.

> Mine tiny, mine all 25, check production cost, and let uORE accumulation be the product.

## New-miner onboarding: curiosity, scams, and the gambling question

A new miner, deflation, arrived with the right first question: “is that basically gambling?” Fronk's answer pattern is worth preserving because it is both protective and educational:

- warn newcomers away from scam DMs immediately;
- point them to the official basics/FAQ before strategy debate;
- distinguish the old proof-of-work era from the current ORE mining game;
- answer the gambling concern directly instead of hand-waving it away;
- invite questions in public where other miners can correct and teach.

The ethos lesson is simple: the channel should make curiosity safe. New miners will ask whether mining is gambling, whether the math has an optimum, why tiny balances appear on every tile, and what changed from older ORE mining. The Almanac should answer those questions calmly, then pull them back to the core path: tiny size, all 25 tiles, production-cost awareness, uORE patience, and public verified links.

> A good mining culture does not just teach settings. It protects newcomers long enough for the settings to make sense.
